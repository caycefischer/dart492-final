Medical Nano-Technology

The near-future emergence of nano-technology is a foregone conclusion; as research in the fields of bio-engineering and computer science grows ever-closer together, nano-technology will undoubtedly revolutionize healthcare (alongside manufacturing, warfare, environmentalism) within our lifetimes. With medical science currently engineering biological ‘machines’ on a nano-scale to fight heart disease and cancer, the leap to Revelation Space-style ‘medbots’ is not a hard one to make.

A Canadian's

average life expectancy

has steadily risen through the 20th century.

This is chiefly due advances in pharmaceutical and surgical technology.

Since the human genome was sequenced in 2003, astonishing breakthroughs have been made in the treatment of humanity's most notorious scourges: heart disease and cancer.

In Canada,

the 5-year survival rate of most cancers has

improved 36.4% since the mid-'70s.

The trend is clear, especially in light of recent breakthroughs in bio-engineered drug delivery systems, which involve designing nano-particles that target specific cancer cells and deliver with pinpoint accuracy antibodies, toxins or drugs.

What would change...

if medical nano-technology was ubiquitous? Millions of medbots could flood a person’s bloodstream - breaking up tumors or lesions, repairing damage surgery could never accomplish. Could we clear clogged arteries? Could we eliminate tumors without open surgery? Could medical nanobots help do the work of our liver and kidneys?

Would we see an end to cancer, or the flu?

Would we live longer? How long?

Since 1950,

the percentage of Canada's population over 65 has

doubled.

The UN expects it to triple by 2050.

If the future promises

radically increased lifespans

& disease prevention,

the UN's estimates are conservative at best.

⦿ The Baby Boomers, who are just now turning 65, may not retire as expected - leaving a generation of young workers unable to find jobs.

⦿ Existing retirees will draw from pension funds longer as lifespans increase, putting stress on an already fragile system.

Trans-Humanism

is a social movement that embraces technologies like nano-biotics, envisioning the nature of humanity's future.

therapy vs enhancement

is a key question Trans-humanism tries to navigate.

Terms like 'health', 'disease', 'therapy' and 'medicine' will have to be radically redefined. Just take one example: once nanotechnology-enabled biosensing techniques are able to identify individual cancer cells inside the body, when do we say that a person is suffering from cancer? Already with one cell (everyone is carrying abnormal cells)? With 100 cells? A million? When does a person's status change from 'healthy' to 'sick'? What implications does that have for, say, the cost of their health insurance?
